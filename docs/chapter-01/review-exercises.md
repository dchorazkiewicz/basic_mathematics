# Review Exercises

## Elementary computational and graphical exercises

1. Plot the points

   $$
   A=(3,2),\qquad B=(-2,4),\qquad C=(-3,-1),\qquad D=(2,-3).
   $$

   For each point, state its quadrant and draw its orthogonal projections onto both axes.

2. For $P=(-4,3)$, write the two projection points on the coordinate axes and compute the distances from $P$ to the $x$-axis, the $y$-axis and the origin.

3. A point has horizontal coordinate $-5$ and vertical coordinate $2$. Construct it from the axes by orthogonal projection. Repeat for $(0,-4)$ and explain what is special about its position.

4. Compute and illustrate with right triangles the distances

   $$
   d((1,2),(4,6)),\qquad d((-2,1),(4,1)),\qquad d((-3,-2),(1,1)).
   $$

5. Find the midpoint of the segment joining $A=(-2,1)$ and $B=(4,5)$. Verify that it is equally distant from both endpoints.

6. A point has coordinates $P=(6,-4)$ when one unit on each axis has its original length. Find its coordinates when one new unit equals two old units and the origin and directions remain unchanged.

7. A point has coordinates $P=(5,3)$. Move the origin to the old point $(2,1)$ without changing the units or axis directions. Compute the new coordinates.

8. A point has coordinates $P=(4,-2)$. Reverse only the positive direction of the $x$-axis. Find the new coordinates and draw both descriptions of the same point.

9. Prepare a value table for $y=-2x+4$ at five convenient integer values of $x$. Plot the points and find the axis intersections.

10. Plot $y=x+2$ and $y=x^2$ in one coordinate system. Find their common points algebraically and verify them in both equations.

## Development and reasoning exercises

1. Describe how to construct a Cartesian coordinate system starting only from a plane, one chosen point, one chosen line through that point and the ability to construct perpendicular lines. Identify every additional choice required before numerical coordinates can be assigned.

2. Explain why a geometric point is not the same object as an ordered pair. Describe one point in two coordinate systems with different origins.

3. Derive

   $$
   d(P,Q)=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}
   $$

   from the horizontal and vertical displacements between $P=(x_1,y_1)$ and $Q=(x_2,y_2)$. Include a diagram and explain where perpendicularity is used.

4. A drawing uses twice the scale horizontally as vertically. Explain why $x^2+y^2=4$ may look like an ellipse even though the equation describes a circle.

5. Find all points on the $x$-axis whose distance from $P=(2,3)$ is $5$. Predict geometrically how many solutions can occur before calculating.

6. A point is described by $(6,2)$. A new coordinate system moves the origin to $(2,-1)$, reverses the $x$-axis direction and uses a unit twice as long. Compute the new coordinates one change at a time.

7. Three vertices of an axis-aligned rectangle are

   $$
   A=(-2,-1),\qquad B=(3,-1),\qquad C=(3,2).
   $$

   Find the fourth vertex and compute side and diagonal lengths.

8. Find the common points of $y=2x-3$ and $y=x^2-2$. Explain why solving $2x-3=x^2-2$ is exactly the algebraic version of asking for points belonging to both curves.

9. Explain why two different points cannot receive the same ordered pair under orthogonal projection and why every ordered pair determines exactly one point.

10. Explain what positions can be labelled after finitely many bisections of the scale and why continuity is needed to assign a real coordinate to every point of the axis.
