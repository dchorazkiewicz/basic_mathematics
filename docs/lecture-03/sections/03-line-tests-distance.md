  <h2>Vector tests behind the slope rules</h2>
  <div class="statement"><div class="statement-label">Direction-vector criteria</div><div class="display-math">$$\boxed{\mathbf v_1\parallel\mathbf v_2\iff \mathbf v_2=\lambda\mathbf v_1\text{ for some }\lambda\neq0},$$</div><div class="display-math">$$\boxed{\mathbf v_1\perp\mathbf v_2\iff \mathbf v_1\cdot\mathbf v_2=0}. $$</div></div>
  <p>These criteria include vertical directions without a special convention. For nonvertical lines take $\mathbf v_1=[1,m_1]$ and $\mathbf v_2=[1,m_2]$. Then</p>
  <div class="derivation"><h3>Recovering the familiar slope rule</h3><div class="display-math">$$\mathbf v_1\cdot\mathbf v_2=1+m_1m_2,$$</div><div class="display-math">$$\boxed{L_1\perp L_2\iff m_1m_2=-1}. $$</div><p>This is the slope form of the dot-product criterion, not an independent rule.</p></div>
  <h2>The nearest point on a line</h2>
  <p>Let $L=\{P_0+t\mathbf v:t\in\mathbb R\}$ with $\mathbf v\neq\mathbf0$, and let $P$ be any point.</p>
  <div class="derivation"><h3>Projecting onto the allowed direction</h3><p>Decompose $P-P_0$ into a component parallel to $\mathbf v$ and a perpendicular component:</p><div class="display-math">$$\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf v}(P-P_0)=\frac{(P-P_0)\cdot\mathbf v}{\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf v}\,\mathbf v.$$</div><p>Starting at $P_0$ and moving by this component reaches</p><div class="display-math">$$\boxed{Q=P_0+\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf v}(P-P_0)}.$$</div></div>
  <figure class="figure-panel jsx-panel" data-fullscreen-panel><div class="figure-toolbar"><div><span class="figure-title">Nearest point and distance to a line</span><span class="figure-step-title">Move $P$, $P_0$, and the line direction.</span></div><button class="icon-button" type="button" data-fullscreen>⛶</button></div><div class="figure-stage jsx-stage"><div id="line-distance-board" class="jxgbox" data-line-distance></div></div><div class="relation-readout"><span data-foot-readout></span><span data-distance-readout></span></div><figcaption class="figure-caption">The remainder $P-Q$ is perpendicular to the permitted direction of the line.</figcaption></figure>
  <div class="statement"><div class="statement-label">Distance in parametric form</div><div class="display-math">$$\boxed{d(P,L)=\left\|(P-P_0)-\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf v}(P-P_0)\right\|}. $$</div></div>
  <p>Every other route from $P$ to the line contains the same perpendicular component plus an additional component along the line. The perpendicular alone is shortest.</p>
  <h3>Distance from the normal equation</h3>
  <p>Suppose $L:ax+by=c$ with normal $\mathbf n=[a,b]$. For $P=(x_P,y_P)$ and any $P_0$ on the line,</p>
  <div class="derivation"><h3>Reading the normal component</h3><div class="display-math">$$\mathbf n\cdot(P-P_0)=ax_P+by_P-c.$$</div><p>The signed scalar component along the normal is $(ax_P+by_P-c)/\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$. Distance is its absolute value.</p></div>
  <div class="statement"><div class="statement-label">Point-to-line distance</div><div class="display-math">$$\boxed{d(P,L)=\frac{|ax_P+by_P-c|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}}. $$</div><p>The numerator measures signed displacement in the normal direction; the denominator normalises the normal vector.</p></div>
