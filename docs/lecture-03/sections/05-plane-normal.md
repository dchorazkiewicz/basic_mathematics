## A normal vector from two directions {#plane-normal}

The plane is generated by two free directions. To write one scalar equation for it, we need a free vector perpendicular to both.

In three-dimensional space the cross product is an additional vector operation:

$$
V\times V\longrightarrow V,
\qquad
(\mathbf u,\mathbf v)\longmapsto\mathbf u\times\mathbf v.
$$

!!! definition "Cross product"
    For $\mathbf u=[u_1,u_2,u_3]$ and $\mathbf v=[v_1,v_2,v_3]$,

    $$
    \boxed{\mathbf u\times\mathbf v=[u_2v_3-u_3v_2,\;u_3v_1-u_1v_3,\;u_1v_2-u_2v_1]}.
    $$

Direct substitution gives

$$
(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)\cdot\mathbf u=0,
\qquad
(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)\cdot\mathbf v=0.
$$

Thus $\mathbf u\times\mathbf v$ is perpendicular to both generating directions and may be used as a normal. Reversing the order reverses the normal,

$$
\mathbf v\times\mathbf u=-(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v),
$$

but both normals describe the same plane.

!!! derivation "From the normal direction to an equation"
    Let

    $$
    \mathbf n=\mathbf u\times\mathbf v.
    $$

    A variable point $P$ belongs to the plane through $P_0$ precisely when the free displacement

    $$
    [P-P_0]
    $$

    lies in the span of $\mathbf u$ and $\mathbf v$. Since $\mathbf n$ is perpendicular to both generating directions,

    $$
    \boxed{\mathbf n\cdot[P-P_0]=0}.
    $$

    Both factors of the dot product are free vectors, so the left-hand side is a real number.

    For $P=(x,y,z)$, $P_0=(x_0,y_0,z_0)$, and $\mathbf n=[a,b,c]$,

    $$
    [P-P_0]=[x-x_0,\,y-y_0,\,z-z_0],
    $$

    hence

    $$
    a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0,
    $$

    or

    $$
    \boxed{ax+by+cz=d}.
    $$

!!! summary "Two descriptions, one plane"
    $$
    \boxed{P=P_0\oplus(s\mathbf u+t\mathbf v)},
    \qquad
    \boxed{\mathbf n\cdot[P-P_0]=0\iff ax+by+cz=d}.
    $$

    The parametric equation first forms an allowed free displacement and applies it to $P_0$. The normal equation requires the forbidden normal component of $[P-P_0]$ to vanish.
