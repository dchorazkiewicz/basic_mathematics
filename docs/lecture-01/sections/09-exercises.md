    <header>
      <span class="exercise-range">1â€“10</span>
      <div>
        <h3 id="technical-exercises">Technical and computational practice</h3>
        <p>Plot, substitute, calculate and verify.</p>
      </div>
    </header>
    <ol>
      <li>Plot the points $A=(-3,2)$, $B=(4,-1)$, $C=(0,5)$ and $D=(-2,-4)$. State the quadrant of each point that does not lie on an axis.</li>
      <li>For $P=(-2,3)$ and $Q=(4,-5)$, compute the horizontal difference, the vertical difference and the Euclidean distance $d_E(P,Q)$.</li>
      <li>For $y=2x+1$, calculate $y$ for $x=-3,-1,0,2,4$, and write the five corresponding points of the graph.</li>
      <li>For $y=\frac{6}{x}$, calculate the points corresponding to $x=-6,-3,-1,1,2,6$. Indicate which inputs are not allowed.</li>
      <li>The circle $x^2+y^2=25$ is intersected by the vertical lines $x=0$, $x=3$, $x=5$ and $x=6$. For each line, find all real values of $y$ and state how many intersection points occur.</li>
      <li>Decide whether $(0,0)$, $(4,0)$, $(2,3)$, $(-1,4)$ and $(5,-2)$ lie inside, on, or outside the disk $x^2+y^2\le16$.</li>
      <li>For $x(t)=3\cos t$, $y(t)=3\sin t$, compute the point for $t=0,\frac{\pi}{2},\pi,\frac{3\pi}{2},2\pi$.</li>
      <li>For $x(t)=t$, $y(t)=t^2-2$, compute the points corresponding to $t=-2,-1,0,1,2$. Verify that every computed point satisfies $y=x^2-2$.</li>
      <li>Find all intersection points of $y=x^2-2$ and $y=x+2$. Give exact coordinates and decimal approximations.</li>
      <li>For each of $(0,0)$, $(0,3)$, $(2,3)$, $(-2,0)$ and $(1,2)$, decide whether it belongs to the region $y\ge x^2-2$, $y\le x+2$.</li>
    </ol>

  <section class="exercise-group exercise-group--problems" aria-labelledby="problem-exercises">
    <header>
      <span class="exercise-range">11â€“20</span>
      <div>
        <h3 id="problem-exercises">Problems requiring explanation and argument</h3>
        <p>Explain why, derive a rule, or justify a geometric conclusion.</p>
      </div>
    </header>
    <ol start="11">
      <li>A Cartesian coordinate system requires an origin, two perpendicular axes, positive directions and a unit length. Explain what information is lost if any one of these choices is omitted. Give a concrete example in each case.</li>
      <li>Prove from the coordinate formula that $d_E(P,Q)=d_E(Q,P)$. Then show that translating both points by the same vector does not change their distance.</li>
      <li>The equation $x^2+y^2=9$ does not define $y$ as a function of $x$ on the whole circle. Explain why. Find two natural restrictions that do define functions and write their formulas.</li>
      <li>For $y=\frac{4}{x}$, explain why the graph has two branches, never meets either coordinate axis, and is symmetric with respect to the origin.</li>
      <li>The parametrization $x(t)=3\cos t$, $y(t)=3\sin t$ uses one parameter to describe a circle. Explain why this makes the circle one-dimensional although it lies in a plane. Is the parameter value associated with a point unique?</li>
      <li>For $P=(p_1,p_2)$ and $Q=(q_1,q_2)$, construct a parametrization of the segment from $P$ to $Q$ using $t\in[0,1]$. Explain the roles of $t=0$, $t=1$ and the intermediate values.</li>
      <li>Determine exactly for which values of $x$ there exist points satisfying both $y\ge x^2-2$ and $y\le x+2$. Use the boundary intersections and explain why the region is bounded.</li>
      <li>For which values of $m$ and $b$ does $y=mx+b$ meet $y=x^2$ in exactly one point? Derive a condition and interpret it geometrically.</li>
      <li>Starting from the distance formula, derive the equation $(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$ of a circle with centre $(h,k)$ and radius $r>0$. Explain why replacing equality by $\le$ gives the disk.</li>
      <li>For the upper half-disk $x^2+y^2\le9$, $y\ge0$ and the region $y\ge x^2-2$, $y\le x+2$, identify the boundary curves and the points satisfying two boundary equations. Decide whether each region is convex and justify your answer.</li>
    </ol>
