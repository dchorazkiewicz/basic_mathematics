Lecture 04 — Conic Sections Through Cartesian and Polar Eyes

Computational and graphical exercises

  1. For the circle \(x^2+y^2=16\), write a trigonometric parameterisation and a polar equation centred at the circle centre. Mark the points corresponding to \(0,\pi/2,\pi,3\pi/2\).
  2. For the ellipse \(x^2/25+y^2/9=1\), find \(a\), \(b\), \(c\), both foci, and the eccentricity. Draw a complete sketch with semiaxes and foci.
  3. A parabola has focus \(F=(2,0)\) and directrix \(x=-2\). Derive its Cartesian equation, find its vertex, and draw the equal-distance construction for one point.
  4. For the hyperbola \(x^2/16-y^2/9=1\), find the vertices, foci, eccentricity, and asymptotes. Draw both branches.
  5. Convert \((\sqrt3,1)\) and \((-1,\sqrt3)\) from Cartesian to polar coordinates, and \((4,\pi/6)\) and \((3,\pi)\) from polar to Cartesian coordinates. Plot all four points.
  6. Convert \(r=6\cos\theta\) to Cartesian form. Identify the centre and radius and explain why it passes through the pole.
  7. For \(r=6/(1+\tfrac12\cos\theta)\), identify \(p\) and \(e\), classify the conic, and compute its nearest and farthest focal distances.
  8. Classify and sketch \(r=3/(1+0.4\cos\theta)\), \(r=3/(1+\cos\theta)\), and \(r=3/(1+1.5\cos\theta)\).
  9. Sketch \(r=4/(1+0.6\cos(\theta-\pi/4))\). Mark the focus, periapsis direction, nearest point, and farthest point.
  10. For \(r=4/(1+2\cos\theta)\), find the angles at which the denominator vanishes, determine where \(r\ge0\), and sketch the traced branch with its asymptotic directions.

Development and reasoning exercises

  1. Draw one point in Cartesian and polar coordinates. Derive \(x=r\cos\theta\), \(y=r\sin\theta\), and \(r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\). Explain why polar coordinates are adapted to one distinguished focus.
  2. Explain why \(r=R\) is simpler than \(x^2+y^2=R^2\) for a circle centred at the pole. Then move the centre and discuss what simplicity is lost.
  3. Starting from \(d(P,F)=e\,d(P,D)\) with the pole at \(F\) and directrix \(x=d\), derive \(r=p/(1+e\cos\theta)\) and \(p=ed\) using a labelled diagram.
  4. Fix \(p=2\) and compare the ellipses for \(e=0.2\), \(e=0.6\), and \(e=0.9\) using the denominator and \(r_{\min},r_{\max}\).
  5. Explain directly from \(r=p/(1+e\cos\theta)\) why \(e=1\) is the transition between a closed ellipse and an open conic.
  6. For \(e>1\) and \(r\ge0\), explain why the focal polar equation traces one branch without making the other branch disappear.
  7. Compare the centre-based ellipse \(x^2/a^2+y^2/b^2=1\) with the focus-based equation \(r=a(1-e^2)/(1+e\cos\theta)\). State what is immediate in each description.
  8. Explain why the parameter \(t\) in \(x=a\cos t\), \(y=b\sin t\) is not generally the polar angle measured from a focus.
  9. An orbit has nearest focal distance \(4\) and farthest focal distance \(12\). Determine \(a\), \(e\), and \(p\), and write its polar equation with periapsis at \(\theta=0\).
  10. Explain separately the roles of \(p\), \(e\), and \(\theta_0\) in \(r=p/(1+e\cos(\theta-\theta_0))\). Summarise why the chapter prepares a later study of orbits in mechanics.
