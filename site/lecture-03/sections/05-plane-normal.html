A normal vector from two directions

The plane is generated by two directions. To write one equation for it, we need a vector perpendicular to both.

Cross product

For $\mathbf u=[u_1,u_2,u_3]$ and $\mathbf v=[v_1,v_2,v_3]$,

$$\boxed{\mathbf u\times\mathbf v=[u_2v_3-u_3v_2,\;u_3v_1-u_1v_3,\;u_1v_2-u_2v_1]}. $$

Direct substitution gives

$$(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)\cdot\mathbf u=0,\qquad(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v)\cdot\mathbf v=0.$$

Thus $\mathbf u\times\mathbf v$ is perpendicular to both generating directions and may be used as a normal. Reversing the order reverses the normal,

$$\mathbf v\times\mathbf u=-(\mathbf u\times\mathbf v),$$

but both normals describe the same plane.

From the normal direction to an equation

Let $\mathbf n=\mathbf u\times\mathbf v$. A point $P$ belongs to the plane exactly when $P-P_0$ is an allowed combination, so

$$\mathbf n\cdot(P-P_0)=0.$$

For $\mathbf n=[a,b,c]$,

$$a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0,$$

or

$$\boxed{ax+by+cz=d}. $$
Two descriptions, one plane
$$\boxed{P=P_0+s\mathbf u+t\mathbf v},\qquad \boxed{\mathbf n\cdot(P-P_0)=0\iff ax+by+cz=d}. $$

The parametric equation lists permitted motions; the normal equation says that the forbidden normal component must vanish.
