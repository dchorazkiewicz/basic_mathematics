Cartesian Geometry and Coordinates

We begin with Euclidean geometry and construct, step by step, the numerical language that turns points into ordered pairs and geometric relations into equations.

Before coordinates

A Euclidean plane already contains points, straight lines, right angles and lengths. Coordinates do not create these objects. They provide a systematic numerical description after we make several explicit choices.

Available geometry

Before coordinates we may construct lines through points, perpendicular lines, circles, intersections and lengths. What is missing is an origin, orientation and numerical scale.

Constructing the Cartesian system

The sequence below distinguishes permanent objects from temporary construction aids. Compass circles and bisectors disappear after they have produced the required mark. Open the figure full screen during a lecture and move through the construction one step at a time.

Construction principle

The scale is not drawn at once. A fixed compass opening is transferred successively along each axis. The interval is then refined by geometric bisection before coordinates are assigned by orthogonal projection.

Assigning coordinates

Let $P$ be any point of the plane. Through $P$, construct perpendiculars to both axes. Their intersection points with the axes determine two signed real numbers $x$ and $y$.

Coordinate correspondence

Once two perpendicular axes, their positive directions and a common unit are fixed, orthogonal projection establishes a one-to-one correspondence between points of the Euclidean plane and ordered pairs $(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2$.

Show the proof
Proof

For every point $P$, the perpendicular to each axis is unique, so the two projections are uniquely determined. Conversely, for fixed real numbers $x$ and $y$, the perpendiculars through the corresponding points on the axes meet in exactly one point. Therefore the correspondence is bijective. $\square$

The coordinate formula for distance

For points $P=(x_1,y_1)$ and $Q=(x_2,y_2)$, the horizontal and vertical separations form the legs of a Euclidean right triangle.

$$ \begin{aligned} d_E(P,Q)^2 &= (x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2,\\ d_E(P,Q) &= \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}. \end{aligned} $$
Interpretation

The formula does not invent distance. It translates the already existing Euclidean length of the segment $PQ$ into arithmetic performed on coordinates.

Equations as geometric descriptions

Once points have numerical addresses, an equation or inequality can select a subset of the plane.

$$ \begin{aligned} x^2+y^2 &= 4 &&\text{circle},\\ x^2+y^2 &\le 4 &&\text{disk},\\ y &= x+2 &&\text{straight line},\\ y &= x^2 &&\text{parabola}. \end{aligned} $$

An intersection point satisfies several conditions simultaneously. For example, solving $x^2=x+2$ gives the common points of the parabola $y=x^2$ and the line $y=x+2$.
