Scalar multiplication and linear combinations

Multiplication by a real number changes the size and possibly the orientation of a vector, but never its direction line.

Scalar multiplication
$$\boxed{t[a_1,a_2]=[ta_1,ta_2].}$$

For $t>0$ the orientation is unchanged. For $t<0$ it is reversed. In every case

$$\|t\mathbf a\|=|t|\,\|\mathbf a\|.$$

Linear combinations

The expression $a\mathbf u+b\mathbf v$ is not a new operation: first scale the two vectors, then add the resulting displacements.

$$\boxed{a\mathbf u+b\mathbf v.}$$
One direction versus two

A single nonzero vector generates only the line $\{\lambda\mathbf v:\lambda\in\mathbb R\}$. Two parallel vectors still generate one line. Two nonparallel vectors provide two independent directions and every vector in the plane has a unique representation

$$\boxed{\mathbf w=a\mathbf u+b\mathbf v.}$$

This is the bridge to a basis: basis coordinates are precisely the coefficients needed to reconstruct a vector from two independent directions.
