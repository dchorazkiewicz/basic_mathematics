The angle between two vectors

The ordinary angle between two nonzero vectors is the smaller, unoriented separation between their directions. It is the unique $\theta\in[0,\pi]$ satisfying

$$\boxed{\cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v}{\|\mathbf u\|\,\|\mathbf v\|}.}$$

The interval $[0,\pi]$ chooses the smaller angle: directions of $10^\circ$ and $350^\circ$ are separated by $20^\circ$, not $340^\circ$.

Principal cases
$$\theta=0\iff\text{same direction},\qquad \theta=\frac\pi2\iff\text{perpendicular},\qquad \theta=\pi\iff\text{opposite directions}.$$

Why the dot product can be expanded

The coordinate definition makes its algebraic behaviour visible. For $\mathbf u=[u_1,u_2]$, $\mathbf v=[v_1,v_2]$, and $\mathbf w=[w_1,w_2]$,

$$\begin{aligned}(\mathbf u+\mathbf w)\cdot\mathbf v&=(u_1+w_1)v_1+(u_2+w_2)v_2\\&=\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v+\mathbf w\cdot\mathbf v,\\(\lambda\mathbf u)\cdot\mathbf v&=\lambda(\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v),\\\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v&=\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf u.\end{aligned}$$

In particular,

$$\boxed{\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf u=\|\mathbf u\|^2.}$$

These identities are not formal rules imported from elsewhere. They are direct consequences of coordinate arithmetic and justify later expansions such as $(\mathbf u-\mathbf v)\cdot(\mathbf u-\mathbf v)$.

The cosine theorem as a vector identity

The triangle with sides $\mathbf u$, $\mathbf v$, and $\mathbf u-\mathbf v$ satisfies

$$\|\mathbf u-\mathbf v\|^2=\|\mathbf u\|^2+\|\mathbf v\|^2-2\|\mathbf u\|\,\|\mathbf v\|\cos\theta.$$

Expanding the left-hand side with the dot product gives the same relation and recovers

$$\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v=\|\mathbf u\|\,\|\mathbf v\|\cos\theta.$$
