From Cartesian points to free vectors

Let $P=(p_1,p_2)$ and $Q=(q_1,q_2)$. Coordinate arithmetic allows us to form

$$Q-P=(q_1-p_1,\,q_2-p_2).$$

At first this is only subtraction of ordered pairs. Its geometric meaning appears when the ordered pair $(P,Q)$ is interpreted as an arrow beginning at $P$ and ending at $Q$.

Anchored vector

The arrow determined by the ordered pair $(P,Q)$ is denoted $\overrightarrow{PQ}$. Its coordinate change is $Q-P$ and its length is the distance between its endpoints:

$$|\overrightarrow{PQ}|=d_E(P,Q).$$

When do two arrows represent the same vector?

Two anchored vectors $\overrightarrow{PQ}$ and $\overrightarrow{P'Q'}$ are equal when they are opposite directed sides of a parallelogram. Equivalently, the diagonals of that parallelogram have a common midpoint:

$$\frac12(P+Q')=\frac12(Q+P').$$

Rearranging gives the coordinate criterion

$$\boxed{\overrightarrow{PQ}=\overrightarrow{P'Q'}\iff Q-P=Q'-P'.}$$
Free vector

A free vector is the entire class of anchored arrows representing the same directed change. If the common coordinate change is $(v_1,v_2)$, we write

$$\mathbf v=[v_1,v_2].$$

Round brackets denote a point; square brackets denote a vector. The point $V=(v_1,v_2)$ is not the vector $\mathbf v=[v_1,v_2]$, although $\overrightarrow{OV}$ is a convenient representative of that vector.
