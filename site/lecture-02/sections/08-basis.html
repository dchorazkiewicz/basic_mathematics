One vector in a different basis

A vector does not change when we describe it in another basis. Only the coefficients used to reconstruct it from the chosen basis vectors change.

For the concrete vector $\mathbf v=[4,2]$, seek coefficients $\alpha$ and $\beta$ such that

$$\mathbf v=\alpha\mathbf b_1+\beta\mathbf b_2,$$

where

$$\mathbf b_1=[1,1],\qquad \mathbf b_2=[1,-1].$$

Coordinate comparison gives

$$[4,2]=[\alpha+\beta,\alpha-\beta],$$

hence

$$\alpha+\beta=4,\qquad \alpha-\beta=2.$$

Solving yields $\alpha=3$ and $\beta=1$, so

$$\boxed{\mathbf v=3\mathbf b_1+\mathbf b_2},\qquad \boxed{[\mathbf v]_B=[3,1].}$$
What changes and what does not

The Cartesian coordinates $[4,2]$ and the basis coordinates $[3,1]_B$ describe the same geometric vector. Changing basis means solving for the coefficients in a chosen linear combination.
